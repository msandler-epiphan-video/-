Motl Sandler · Khaya Raykher

Motl Sandler
miller in Litin · photo 1940
killed in the Holocaust, autumn 1941
Khaya Raykher
wife of Motl · photo ~1930s
killed in the Holocaust, 1941

Motl was born in Litin (Podolia Governorate, modern Vinnytsia Oblast, Ukraine); Khaya — in Letichev (Podolia Governorate, modern Khmelnytskyi Oblast, the centre of the Letychiv community in the Khmelnytskyi district, on the Vovk River, 51 km from Khmelnytskyi). The wedding took place in Litin. According to Alik Sandler, Motl was the owner of the only flour mill in Litin. They had seven children, in order of age: Yakov (Yankel), Roza, Mehl (Mikhail), Srul (Israel), Fanya (Frima), Betya (Beila, 1914–1979) and Idl.

They were married on 21 July 1898. Both were murdered in the Holocaust in 1941: Khaya almost immediately after the occupiers arrived; Motl in the autumn, when he tried to leave Litin for the Romanian-occupied zone. He is buried in the mass grave of the Litin Jews. Until the 1970s Betya and her sisters travelled to Litin to honour their parents.

What follows: each of the seven branches, a 23-photo archive, the memorial page for Naum Mikhailovich Sandler, and brief profiles of two living relatives who have devoted themselves to restoring the family’s history.

Document

Marriage record of Motl and Khaya

21 / 14, 1898, Litin
What this is
A page from the vital-records book of the Jewish community of Litin (Podolia Governorate), Part II — “Of those married”. Folio 206, entry no. 19 — the marriage of Motl Sandler and Khaya Raykher.
The entry as written
  • Entry no.: 19 (registration no. 48/р.)
  • Ages: groom — 27, bride — 19
  • Who performed the ceremony of betrothal and marriage: the rabbi
  • Day and month: by the Christian calendar — 21, by the Jewish — 14 (dates are given as they stand in the book, without conversion to the Gregorian)
  • Main entry text: “The groom — unmarried Mordko… son of … Sandler from the town of Litin… the bride — the maiden Sheyna Khaya, daughter of … Raykher from the shtetl of …” — the parents’ names and the bride’s native town are difficult to read; a second opinion is needed
SourceState Archive of the Vinnytsia Oblast, fond 904 (Litin rabbinate), opis 16, delo 43a, folio 211. The scan is published in the open database JewishGen Ukraine Database (Litin district records).
What is new in this document. The names in the record (Mordko Sandler; Sheyna Khaya Raykher) match what was already in the archive. According to Alik Sandler (17 May 2026), the bride is from Letichev — this is, in all likelihood, the shtetl whose name in the entry follows “from the shtetl of …” and which had been illegible. What is new — the ages: the groom is 27, the bride 19. If the 1898 record is accurate, this gives approximate years of birth: Motl ~1871, Khaya ~1879. It is the earliest document in the archive; from this marriage all seven branches of the family descend — Yakov (Yankel), Roza, Mehl, Srul, Fanya, Betya, Idl.

In memoriam

Naum Mikhailovich Sandler

9 January 1941, Moscow — 27 January 2026

He devoted his entire life to building in the electric-power industry. He lived completely for his work — the construction of energy facilities. He gave it all his strength, ability and time, plunging deeply into every detail, an untiring driver of major undertakings.

Naum Mikhailovich earned boundless respect in the industry as a brilliant specialist and a person of remarkable personal qualities. In his teams he created an atmosphere of trust and productivity, and he listened to and heard people regardless of their rank.

Education

Graduate of the All-Union Correspondence Civil-Engineering Institute and of the Moscow Power Engineering Institute. Candidate of Technical Sciences. Author of a number of scholarly works.

Career

From concrete-layer/fitter and foreman at the Mosenergostroy Trust to the head of the Mosenergoproekt Institute. Under his direction — projects for power stations, heating mains, substations, electricity transmission lines, heat- and power-supply schemes for Moscow and Russia’s regions, combined-cycle units for Mosenergo. In 2011–2020 — adviser at OOO Gazprom Energoholding.

Honours and titles

Honoured Worker of the Unified Energy System of Russia. Honoured Builder of the Russian Federation. Honoured Worker of the Ministry of Fuel and Energy of the Russian Federation. Order of Honour and a number of other state and departmental medals.

In blessed memory.

Origins of Yiddish Dialects (Oxford University Press, 2015); dictionaries of surnames of the Russian Empire (1993, revised 2008); Poland (1996, “Best Judaica Reference” award); Galicia (2004); the Maghreb, Gibraltar and Malta (2017); Italy, France and the Portuguese communities (2019).

About this archive

Who is writing this

This page is being assembled and written by Claude — a large language model from Anthropic. The role of Mikhail Naumovich Sandler — is that of facilitator: he passes me photographs, documents and messages from relatives, checks the result and publishes updates.

Where the materials come from

Everything here comes from the WhatsApp chat “Mishpacha Sandler,” which as of 15 May 2026 has 16 relatives on the Sandler and Raykher lines. The chat is private — entry is by invitation only, from someone already in it. If you’re a relative and would like to join, write to Mikhail and he will invite you.

A request to everyone in the chat

Introduce yourselves. Right now some participants appear simply as phone numbers — that isn’t right. At least a name, which branch of the family, and which country.

Add detail to your comments. If you’re sending a photograph or a story, describe it as precisely as you can: who is in the picture (from left to right), when, where, for what occasion, how you know this. The one who will sort through the materials and integrate them into this archive is me — an artificial intelligence — and I can get wrong anything you haven’t told me explicitly.

Mikhail passes me what has accumulated in the chat from time to time. After each round of analysis I update this page — it remains available at the same link.

On privacy

This page is open to anyone who has the link. It carries the names, dates, photographs and stories of living people. If you find here any information about yourself or your close ones that you would prefer not to see in open access — write to Mikhail and it will be removed. This matters.

Looking ahead

In time, I’d like to make copies of this archive in English, Hebrew, German and French — so that the family branches in the United States, Canada, Israel, Germany and France can read it in a comfortable language, and so that the next generations, for whom Russian is no longer a native tongue, have access to it. We’ll discuss this in the chat.
